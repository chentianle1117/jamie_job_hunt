### Q1: An employee reaches out frustrated about a benefits issue you don't immediately know the answer to. How would you handle it?

First, I'd acknowledge their frustration right away—something like "I hear you, and I want to make sure we get this resolved." Not knowing the answer immediately doesn't mean leaving them in a loop. I'd tell them specifically what I'm going to do: "Let me look into this and loop in our benefits vendor—I'll get back to you by end of day." Then I'd actually do it.

From my time at Vestas supporting the HRBP team, I learned that employee relations cases need to be handled with both speed and care. You can't over-promise, but you absolutely cannot go quiet on someone who's already frustrated. My approach is to set a clear timeline, document the issue, escalate to the vendor or the right internal contact, and follow up with the employee before they have to chase me. If the resolution takes longer than expected, I'd send an interim update so they know it hasn't been forgotten. The goal is for them to feel heard and informed at every step—not just eventually resolved.

### Q2: What's one small process improvement you've made in a previous role that made things run more smoothly?

At InGenius Prep, new-hire onboarding was disjointed—different team leads had their own versions of how to bring someone up to speed, and new hires often didn't know what they were supposed to know by the end of week one. I noticed the pattern after a few rounds of feedback and proposed consolidating everything into a single onboarding guide: a structured document that covered product knowledge, team processes, key contacts, and where to find what.

It sounds simple, but the impact was real. We went from variable, word-of-mouth onboarding to a consistent baseline that reduced ramp-up time by 75%. More importantly, managers stopped getting the same repeat questions. The improvement wasn't technically complex—it was about identifying a coordination gap, getting buy-in from a few key people, and actually building the thing instead of just talking about it. That "just build the thing" instinct is something I bring to any process problem I encounter.

### Q3: Describe a situation where something could have fallen through the cracks but didn't because of you. What did you do?

During the M&A integration project at Vestas, I was supporting the HR team in migrating and merging employee records across two entities. There were multiple stakeholders involved—legal, HR, and the acquired company's local team—and the process had a lot of moving parts with a hard compliance deadline.

At one point I noticed that a subset of records for employees in a specific country had incomplete data fields that didn't match the target system's requirements. No one had flagged it because each team assumed another team had handled it. I caught it during a cross-check I'd built into my own tracking process, surfaced it immediately to the HRBP lead, and we were able to correct the records before the cutover date.

What I learned from that experience: when there are multiple handoffs in a process, the places most likely to fail are the gaps between teams. I now build explicit ownership checkpoints into any process I'm managing, so that "someone else will handle it" never becomes the default answer.

### Q4: Tell us about a time you were responsible for keeping data, schedules, or processes accurate. What was your approach, and how did you ensure nothing was missed?

At InGenius Prep, I managed the scheduling and logistics for training webinars that reached 600+ participants, alongside coordinating 20+ active programs and 10+ vendor relationships. Accuracy mattered—a missed scheduling confirmation or an outdated process document could cascade into real problems for participants or internal teams.

My approach was systematic: I maintained a running tracking document in Notion where every program had its own row with dates, owners, vendor contacts, and status. I did a weekly audit of all active programs to catch any items that had stalled or weren't updated. For data accuracy specifically, I built a habit of doing a final check before any communication went out—confirming dates, names, links, and contacts against the source of truth rather than assuming.

I also got comfortable saying "let me verify that before I confirm" rather than answering from memory. In a role where accuracy is the baseline expectation, it's better to take 5 extra minutes to be right than to move fast and create downstream corrections.

### Q5: You're juggling onboarding logistics for a new hire, scheduling interviews, and responding to employee questions at the same time. How would you prioritize your work?

My first move is to sort by deadline and consequence. Onboarding logistics for a new hire have a fixed date—if day one isn't ready, that affects a real person's first impression of the company and can't be undone. That goes to the top. Interview scheduling has a time-sensitive component too, especially if candidates are waiting; I'd handle any same-day scheduling needs next. Employee questions I'd triage by urgency—an employee blocked on something gets a quick response (even just "I'm on it—will get back to you by 3pm"), while questions that can wait 30 minutes can batch.

At InGenius, juggling multiple competing priorities was the daily norm—I was managing live program logistics, vendor coordination, and internal communication simultaneously. What kept everything moving was a real-time task list with time-boxed slots, clear communication to stakeholders about when they'd hear back, and a habit of front-loading the irreversible items first. The goal isn't to do everything at once—it's to make sure the things that can't slip, don't.
