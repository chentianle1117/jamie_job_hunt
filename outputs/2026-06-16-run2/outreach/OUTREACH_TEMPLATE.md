# Jamie's Post-Application Outreach — Reusable Template
_Version 1.0 — 2026-06-16_

> Ground rules (always apply):
> - Verify the recipient CURRENTLY works at the company via live LinkedIn before sending.
> - No direct referral ask in the first message. Open with curiosity, not need.
> - Sponsorship: mention work authorization only near the end of an email, matter-of-factly. Never lead with it.
> - No fabricated "I've been following your org's work" language.
> - ODN Oregon = OD diagnostic consulting (never "community building" or "ERG").

## (a) Email to Hiring Manager / People Team Member

**Subject:** {Role Title} at {Company} — Jamie Cheng

```
Hi {FirstName},

{ROLE FUNCTION} isn't just about {surface-level expectation}—from my experience, it's about
{deeper truth: diagnosing root causes / building feedback loops / designing solutions that stick}.

I recently applied for the {Role Title} role at {Company} and wanted to reach out personally.
I was particularly drawn to {ONE specific thing from the JD or what the team does—not the company's
subsidiary structure}. It aligns closely with work I've done in {MATCHING EXPERIENCE AREA—e.g.,
"designing and running cross-functional learning programs for 600+ participants" / "conducting
stakeholder needs assessments to inform program strategy"}.

I have a background in HR, Organizational Development, and Program Management, with an M.S. in
Applied Organizational Psychology from USC. {Optional: 1 sentence with a specific, verifiable proof
point relevant to the role—e.g., "I've reduced onboarding time by 75% by standardizing workflows
and documentation" or "I've facilitated live training for 600+ participants and measured outcomes
via feedback loops."}

I would love to connect for a brief 15-minute chat in the next two weeks if you're open to it—I'd
genuinely value hearing what the day-to-day looks like on your team.

{SPONSORSHIP NOTE — include only if natural context: "I should also note that I'm currently on an
H-1B and would need sponsorship—I wanted to be upfront so it's not a surprise later on."}

I've attached my resume for reference. Thank you so much for your time, {FirstName}!

Warmly,
Jamie (Yi-Chieh) Cheng
jamiecheng0103@gmail.com | 213-700-3831
```

**Customize:**
- Hook sentence: swap the role-function noun (L&D / People Ops / HR Operations) and the deeper truth
- The specific JD detail: pull one real thing from the job description — never generic
- Matching experience: choose the proof point most relevant to this role from the verified list below
- Sponsorship line: include only if the company is known to sponsor or you want to screen early

## (b) Email / LinkedIn InMail to Alumni / Warm Connection

**Subject:** Fellow {USC/Wesleyan} alum — quick question about {Company}

```
Hi {FirstName},

This is Jamie—a fellow {USC/Wesleyan} alum! I came across your profile and noticed you're at
{Company} working in {their function}. {1 sentence about something specific from their profile—
a project, a recent post, their career arc—not generic flattery.}

I recently applied for the {Role Title} role there and thought I'd reach out. As someone
earlier in my career, I'd love to hear about your experience on the team—what excites you
about the work, and any insights you'd be willing to share about the culture or the role.

I have some time in the next two weeks if you'd be open to a 15-minute chat!

Thank you in advance—hope to connect soon!

Warmly,
Jamie Cheng
```

**Customize:**
- Shared-school anchor: USC (MAPP alum ✌️) or Wesleyan ("fellow Wes alum")
- Profile detail: read their LinkedIn before drafting — extract one real, specific thing
- Never ask for a referral directly; frame as "I'd love to hear your experience"

## Sponsorship — How to Handle It

- **Do not open with it.** It is not the first thing Jamie says about herself.
- **In an email:** Include the sponsorship note near the end as one sentence (see template above),
  only if the company is unknown/unverified on H-1B or you want to screen early to save everyone's time.
- **In a LinkedIn note:** Do not mention it. Too short a format; it reads as leading with a liability.
  Address it naturally if the conversation progresses.
- **If directly asked:** "Yes, I'm currently on an H-1B and would need sponsorship. I wanted to be
  upfront about that — happy to discuss further."
