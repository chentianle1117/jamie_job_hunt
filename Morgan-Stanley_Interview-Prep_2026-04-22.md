## How a Hiring Manager Interview Is Different

**What they're evaluating:**
1. Can you do the actual work? (competency — STAR stories)
2. Will you be a good teammate? (chemistry + fit)
3. Do you understand what this role actually is? (beyond the job title)
4. How do you think? (judgment, problem-solving, questions you ask)
5. Will you stay? (motivation — why this team, why now)

**What to DO differently vs. a recruiter call:**
- Be conversational, not "interview mode" — peer-to-peer, not candidate-to-approver
- Expect 4-6 deep-dive behavioral / STAR questions (not surface-level screening)
- Expect hypothetical / role-simulation questions ("How would you approach X at MS?")
- Take notes during their answers — shows attentiveness and gives you material for follow-ups
- Use "we" framing when discussing the role ("how we might approach X") — subtle but signals ownership
- Follow up on specifics they mention — proves you're listening, not running a script

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't lead with visa, salary, or location questions (save for HR/recruiter later)
- Don't treat it like a filter interview — this person may be your boss
- Don't run out the clock on one STAR — they want to see range
- Don't arrive without substantive questions prepared (biggest red flag for hiring managers)

### Change Management Model Cheat Sheet

**ADKAR (individual-level change — Prosci)**
- **A — Awareness** of the need for change
- **D — Desire** to support and participate
- **K — Knowledge** of how to change
- **A — Ability** to implement new skills and behaviors
- **R — Reinforcement** to sustain the change

*Use when:* individual behavior change matters (adoption, training uptake)

**Kübler-Ross Change Curve (emotional response to change)**

Originally for grief, adapted for org change. 5 (sometimes 7) emotional stages people move through:

1. **Shock/Denial** — "This doesn't apply to me" / "It won't actually happen"
2. **Anger/Frustration** — "Why are they doing this to us?"
3. **Bargaining** — "Maybe I can keep doing things the old way for now"
4. **Depression** — "This is overwhelming, I can't keep up"
5. **Acceptance** — "Okay, I need to figure this out"
6. *(Extended)* **Experimentation** — trying new behaviors
7. *(Extended)* **Integration** — the new way is now normal

*Use when:* explaining why people seem to "regress" or push back emotionally; helps you not take resistance personally and meet people where they are

**Kotter's 8 Steps (large-scale org change)**
1. Create urgency
2. Build a guiding coalition
3. Form strategic vision
4. Enlist volunteers / champions
5. Enable action by removing barriers
6. Generate short-term wins
7. Sustain acceleration
8. Institute change

*Use when:* enterprise-wide transformation (like a firmwide AI rollout)

**How to talk about them in the interview:**
- Don't lead with frameworks — lead with diagnosis
- If asked, name 2-3 and briefly say which you've used
- Always end with: "I use frameworks as a lens, not a script"

## Section 3: Performance Measurement & Optimization

### Opening (Honest + Strong)
- "I'll be honest — I haven't owned end-to-end program performance measurement as a formal role, but I've done a lot of adjacent data work, and I have strong opinions on how it should be done because of my psychology background."

### Philosophy (Lead With This)
- Most L&D measurement stops at activity — attendance, completions — but the real question is **did behavior actually change?**
- I approach measurement as research design: define what success means *before* you start collecting data
- Pre/post comparisons with clearly defined outcome variables
- Mixed methods — numbers tell you *what*, interviews tell you *why*

### Primary Example — InGenius Culture-Building Program

**Situation**
- HR was launching a cross-functional connection program
- 2-3 employees matched each month across different functions
- Goal: build culture + encourage cross-team communication

**Task**
- I wasn't running measurement formally, but I was asked to help think through how we'd know if it worked

**Action**
- Started with the question: what does "culture building" actually mean here?
- Proposed defining outcome variables before collecting data:
  - Frequency of cross-team communication (behavioral)
  - Understanding of org structure (cognitive)
  - Sense of belonging (affective)
- Suggested pre/post survey design so we could compare, not just report activity
- Grounded the approach in mixed-methods research design from my psych background

**Result**
- Framed measurement as outcomes-first, not activity-first
- Positioned HR to make a real judgment about program effectiveness, not just say "X people participated"

### Secondary Example — NextGen (Measurement That Changed Strategy)
- Analyzed 2,000+ employee survey responses at NextGen Healthcare
- Goal: identify what actually drives engagement
- Found manager effectiveness was the key lever
- Reported findings → C-suite shifted L&D strategy toward manager training
- *This is measurement upstream:* measuring what *should* be optimized, not just whether a program worked

### Closer — How I'd Apply This at Morgan Stanley

For AI tool adoption measurement, I'd look at:
- **Behavioral signals** — are people actually using the tools after training? Tool usage logs, frequency, depth of use
- **Outcomes** — self-reported time saved, quality of outputs, confidence levels
- **Qualitative** — focus groups and interviews to understand *why* adoption is or isn't happening
- **Not just completion rates** — completion is a lagging activity metric, not a success metric

**Key line to land:**
- "Completion tells you people showed up. Behavior tells you the program worked. My job is to measure the second one."

### Insight to Flag

### GenAI Risks — Practitioner POV

**Your honest authenticity hook:**
- "I use these tools every day — I've hit hallucinations, seen bias in outputs, and had moments where I caught myself about to paste something I shouldn't into a public tool. That practitioner experience is what I'd bring. I don't have to theorize about what fails — I've seen it."

**The 5 main risks to know:**
1. **Hallucinations** — AI confidently generates false info. In finance: incorrect regulatory refs, fabricated data, made-up product specs.
2. **Bias** — training data biases in outputs. Stereotyped language in client comms; uneven recommendations.
3. **Data privacy** — users pasting confidential client data into external tools. Why MS builds internal tools, but users still need training.
4. **Over-reliance** — skipping critical thinking because "AI said so." Especially dangerous in regulated industries where advisors are legally accountable.
5. **Regulatory exposure** — incorrect AI outputs could create SEC/FINRA violations.

### How I'd Approach GenAI Risk Training

1. **Preface every training with context** — before teaching how to use a tool, teach what it can and CAN'T do
2. **Use real examples of failures** — actual hallucination samples, bias examples, privacy near-misses (memorable > abstract)
3. **Build guardrails into the content itself** — "always verify X," "never paste Y," "flag Z"
4. **Teach escalation** — when to go to compliance, when to stop using the tool
5. **Partner with legal/compliance SMEs** — they own the risk content, I own the pedagogy
